
# Chris Walker — What Open Source Should Mean

## Core Idea

Open source is not simply software with public code.

It is a philosophy built on trust, freedom, and community.

When projects prioritize profit over these principles, they stop behaving like true open-source projects.

---

## The Main Argument

Chris argues that many modern open-source projects have drifted away from their original purpose.

Instead of serving users and communities, some projects increasingly resemble traditional software companies.

This creates tension between:

- Commercial growth.
- Community values.
- User trust.
- Long-term independence.

---

## The Five Principles of Open Source

### 1. Security

Open source creates security through:

- Transparency.
- Public code review.
- Community auditing.
- Regular updates.
- Faster patching.

However, security also means reducing dependency risk.

Questions to ask:

- What happens if the project disappears?
- What if development suddenly stops?
- Am I overly dependent on a single vendor?

---

### 2. Stability

Stable projects have:

- Long-term momentum.
- Active maintainers.
- Reliable ecosystems.
- Community support.

Users need confidence that the software will continue to exist.

Stability reduces the risk of sudden product changes, cancellations, or restrictions.

---

### 3. Availability

Open source should remain:

- Affordable.
- Accessible.
- Easy to adopt.

Large projects should also allow:

- Multiple hosting providers.
- Easy migration.
- No vendor lock-in.

Availability gives users freedom of choice.

---

### 4. Flexibility

Open source enables:

- Customization.
- Source code access.
- Integrations.
- Extensions.
- Adaptation to unique business needs.

Users can modify the software rather than adapting their business to the software.

---

### 5. Community

The community is the foundation of open source.

Strong communities provide:

- Contributions.
- Innovation.
- Feedback.
- Documentation.
- Long-term sustainability.

The best projects encourage:

- Decentralized leadership.
- Diverse contributions.
- Collaboration over control.

---

## The Core Concern

Some projects:

- Prioritize monetization.
- Restrict access.
- Limit contributions.
- Centralize decision-making.
- Ignore community feedback.

When that happens, they may technically remain open source.

But they stop acting like open-source communities.

---

## Software Lesson

Users choose open source for more than cost savings.

They choose it for:

- Independence.
- Transparency.
- Control.
- Trust.
- Community.

Losing these qualities can damage long-term adoption.

---

## Memorable Quotes

> "Open source is built on security, stability, availability, flexibility, and community."

> "Community trust and independence should never be sacrificed."

> "Projects that forget their community lose what made them valuable."

> "Open source is more than a licensing model."

---

# Framework for Evaluating Open Source Projects

Ask:

### Security
- Is the project actively maintained?

### Stability
- Will it still exist in five years?

### Availability
- Can I switch providers?

### Flexibility
- Can I customize it?

### Community
- Is the ecosystem healthy?

---

# Broader Business Lesson

Growth and monetization are not inherently bad.

But sustainable ecosystems balance:

- Business interests.
- User interests.
- Community interests.

Trust is difficult to build and easy to lose.

---

# One-Sentence Takeaway

True open source is not defined by access to code.

It is defined by trust, freedom, and community ownership.
